# STAR Method

The STAR method is a structure for answering behavioral interview questions. It forces you to stay concrete, stay on your own work, and end with measurable impact instead of wandering into opinions or generalities.

## Structure

**S — Situation.** Context. Where, when, what team, what was going on. 2–3 sentences max.

**T — Task.** Your specific responsibility or the problem you owned. Not what the team was doing — what *you* were doing. 1–2 sentences.

**A — Action.** What you actually did. This is the bulk of the answer (60–70% of your time). Be specific about your decisions, tradeoffs, and the moments where you could have done something different but chose this. Use "I" far more than "we."

**R — Result.** The outcome, ideally quantified. What changed because of your action? Include what you learned if the result wasn't pure success.

## Common failure modes

- **"We" instead of "I."** Interviewers want to know what *you* did. Narrate the team's role briefly, then zoom in on your own contribution.
- **Too much Situation.** 90 seconds of context, 15 seconds of action. Flip it.
- **No conflict or tradeoff.** A good story has a decision point where you could have gone two ways and you chose. Stories with no decision feel like reports, not experiences.
- **No Result.** "And then the project launched" is not a result. Numbers, behavior changes, follow-on decisions — something concrete.
- **Generic answers.** "I'm a hard worker" is not a STAR story. A STAR story is "In Q3 2024 we had an incident where…"

## Timing

Good STAR answers run 2–3 minutes. Under 90 seconds usually means you skipped action or result. Over 4 minutes means you wandered.

## Levels

The same question demands different depth at different levels:

| Level | Situation / Task scope | Action depth | Result scope |
|---|---|---|---|
| Junior | Individual task within a team's project | Describe your implementation and what you learned | Task-level outcome |
| Mid | Ownership of a component or workstream | Decisions and tradeoffs at a design level; some dependency management | Component-level outcome; metrics if possible |
| Senior | Ownership of a multi-component effort or cross-team coordination | Strategic decisions, tradeoffs between competing goals, influence without authority | Team or org-level outcome; hard metrics |
| Staff | System- or org-level impact | Architectural decisions, cultural shifts, setting direction | Business-level outcome; long-term impact |

A junior story about a bugfix won't hit for a senior role. A staff-level story about "driving alignment across four teams" will sound fake for a junior role. Match the story to the target.

## Common themes interviewers probe

Each one is a category you should have 1–2 stories for:

- **Conflict** — disagreement with a peer, manager, or stakeholder
- **Failure** — a thing you got wrong, and what you did about it
- **Ownership** — something you drove end-to-end without being told to
- **Ambiguity** — unclear requirements, you chose a direction
- **Technical tradeoff** — a decision with real consequences either way
- **Leadership / influence** — moving a group without formal authority (senior+)
- **Mentorship** — helping someone else grow (mid+)
- **Scope change** — the project changed mid-flight, you adapted
- **Cross-functional collaboration** — working with non-engineers
- **Tight deadline** — compressed timeline, you delivered (or cut scope well)
